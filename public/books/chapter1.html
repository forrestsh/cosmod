1.1 The Point of Departure

Why does the universe exist? What is its most fundamental structure? Where does consciousness come from, and where does it go? These questions span three intellectual traditions — physics, philosophy, and theology — each of which has long pursued its own answers in relative isolation from the others.

This book proposes a unified conceptual framework — Cosmic Grid — that attempts to address the core concerns of all three traditions within a single language. This is not an exercise in compromise, but in integration: beginning from an explicit metaphysical starting point, and unfolding from it a physical model, a theological mapping, and a theory of consciousness that together form an internally consistent cosmological framework.

1.2 The Book's Position and Contribution

This book's metaphysical position is unambiguous: the Creator exists, transcends mathematics, and is the designer, initiator, and sustainer of the universe. From this premise, the book argues that the physical substrate of the universe is a discrete information-processing grid on a face-centered cubic (FCC) lattice, with each node processing 12 binary signals; that consciousness is a second type of node, independent of matter, holding unique reaction rules, and constituting a local reflection of the Creator's own consciousness; and that human free will is real, though constrained by three layers of finitude.

The book's core contribution is to integrate, for the first time, a concrete discrete physical substrate, voluntarist theology, and an independent consciousness ontology within a single framework — and to provide a rigorous information-theoretic basis for discreteness through the Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem.

1.3 Structure of the Book

The book comprises eight chapters. Chapter 2 lays the metaphysical foundations. Chapter 3 describes the physical model. Chapter 4 maps the theological and physical layers onto each other. Chapter 5 develops the theory of consciousness. Chapter 6 engages with existing theories. Chapter 7 identifies open problems. Chapter 8 offers conclusions. Readers may proceed sequentially, or enter directly at Chapter 3 or Chapter 5.
